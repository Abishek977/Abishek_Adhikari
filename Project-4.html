Spatio-Temporal Evolution of the Urban Heat Island: A Case Study of Butwal, Nepal

Analyzing the rise of urban temperatures from 2014 to 2023 using geospatial technologies.

Project Overview

This project analyzes the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect in Butwal City, Nepal, over a period of nearly a decade. Using geospatial data from 2014 to 2023, we track the increasing land surface temperature (LST) and identify the factors that contribute to the urban heat problem. This analysis reveals how rapid urbanization has led to higher temperatures, especially in built-up areas, and discusses possible mitigation strategies.

Key Objectives

  • Assess the spatial and temporal changes in land surface temperature (LST).
  • Identify the major drivers of the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect in Butwal.
  • Provide data-driven recommendations for urban planning and heat mitigation.

Data & Tools Used

Technologies and Methods

  • Geographic Information Systems (GIS): Mapping and spatial data analysis
  • Remote Sensing: Using Landsat-8 satellite data
  • Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI): Analyzing vegetation cover
  • Normalized Difference Building Index (NDBI): Assessing building density
  • Land Surface Temperature (LST) Inversion Models: Temperature mapping and trend analysis

Data Sources

  • Landsat-8 Satellite Images (2014, 2017, 2020, 2023)
  • USGS (United States Geological Survey)
  • Butwal City Land Use Data
  • Climate Data (Temperature, Precipitation)

Time Frame

Study period: 2014 - 2023

Primary analysis years: 2014, 2017, 2020, 2023

Analysis & Findings

Urban Heat Island Growth

The analysis showed a significant rise in land surface temperatures (LST) in Butwal, especially in urbanized areas. From 2014 to 2023, the average temperature increased from 30.91°C to 32.09°C, with the highest temperature recorded in built-up areas. This trend was closely tied to the expansion of impervious surfaces and the reduction of vegetative cover.

Vegetation vs. Built-Up Areas

Through NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) and NDBI (Normalized Difference Building Index), it was found that areas with more vegetation consistently had lower temperatures, while urbanized areas experienced the most significant heat increases. The correlation between LST and urbanization is clear: more buildings lead to more heat.

Conclusions & Recommendations

The study underscores the importance of sustainable urban planning to mitigate the effects of the Urban Heat Island (UHI) in Butwal. Strategies like increasing green spaces, encouraging green roofs, and utilizing reflective materials in construction can help reduce the heat. Without intervention, temperatures are expected to rise, leading to negative impacts on public health, energy consumption, and the environment.
